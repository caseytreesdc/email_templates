CASEY TREES TESTIFIES ON D.C. AGENCIES PERFORMANCE

We may only be three months into the year, but the advocacy team has been busy! For the past 5 weeks, Casey Trees staff has been testifying on District agencies’ performance for the 2021 Fiscal Year. This year, Casey Trees commented on the performance of 12 different agencies, advocating for three main things: making sure all trees have equal protection under the law, giving every student in the District access to environmental education, and preserving land in order to build a more resilient DC.

In addition to submitting 12 sets of comments, Casey Trees staff attended eight hearings. In February, we testified at DOEE  and DDOT, asking the Council to amend the Urban Forest Preservation Act to protect all trees with a circumference of 44 inches or larger and give the Urban Forestry Division (UFD) the ability to stop construction if a tree is being harmed. We also advocated to Councilmember Robert White at the Committee on Government Operations and Facilities’ OCTO Performance Oversight hearing for zoning application information to be easier to access and easier to understand.

In March, we testified about the performance of DGS, Office of Planning,  Office of Zoning, and OSSE, advocating for the Zoning Code to be updated to create a “forever green” zoning area, for better coordination between the government and contractors on capital projects and for students across the District to have access to outdoor education.

All in all, it was an eventful and successful advocacy effort. We would like to thank all CTAs who participated in this year’s performance oversight hearings. Because of our advocacy during the performance oversight hearing, we are now talking with the D.C. Council to amend the Urban Forest Preservation Act and DOEE to update the Green Area Ratio.

WOMEN IN ENVIRONMENTAL ADVOCACY: BRENDA RICHARDSON

In honor of Women’s History Month, the Casey Trees Advocacy Team wanted to highlight the amazing women in D.C. who work to restore, enhance, and protect their local environment. 

Brenda Richardson, a self identified eco-feminist, has been an environmental. and community advocate for over 30 years. 

“I have a friend who is an eco-feminist who said that there were few people of color represented in environmental advocacy. After a few meetings, I was hooked”

Folks are dissuaded and dispirited and the environment is not a priority when trying to keep food on the table, Brenda tells Casey Trees, and that is the inspiration for her work - to be a voice for underserved communities when it comes to environmental protection. She uses that passion to drive her work: as former deputy chief of staff for former Councilmember Marion Berry and former board member for Anacostia Watershed Society, to her current work with Friends of Oxon Run and partnerships with UFD, MPD, Capital Nature and more. Her most recent projects include partnering with Casey Trees to do the Ward 8 Tree Challenge, working with UFD and Moms Demand Action to plant memory forests across the District to honor people lost to gun violence and hosting Trauma Breaks, a forest bathing experience for adults at Oxon Run Park that uses the mental health benefits of trees to help with trauma recovery.

“Trees are a blessing for underserved communities who have been peppered with hardship…[that is why] planting trees and connecting to the community is so important.”

FUN TREE FACTS

In honor of cherry blossom season, here are some fun facts about D.C.’s cherry trees!

  • D.C. has 12 different types of cherry trees.
  • Kanzan Cherries are unique in that they can have 28 petals per flower when normal cherry blossoms have five.
  • Every ANC has at least 20 cherry trees in it.
